## What this domain actually tests

Laptops and mobile hardware — the most practical domain on the exam, and the most intuitive if you have ever opened a laptop.

**Laptop component replacement** is the core: RAM (**SO-DIMM**, not the desktop DIMM), storage (2.5" SATA, **M.2**), batteries, keyboards, and display assemblies. Questions describe an upgrade or a repair and ask what part is needed or what the correct procedure is.

**Display technologies** and the components behind the panel — the **inverter** and **backlight** on older LCDs, **digitiser** on touchscreens, and the **Wi-Fi antenna** that typically runs *through the display bezel* (which is why a laptop's wireless can break after a screen replacement — a genuinely favourite exam scenario).

**Mobile connectivity**: Bluetooth pairing, **NFC**, hotspots and tethering, and synchronisation.

## The traps

**Antenna placement in the lid.** If a scenario says wireless stopped working after a display was replaced, the antenna cable was not reconnected. This is one of the domain's signature questions and it seems obscure until you have seen it once.

**Digitiser versus LCD versus inverter.** If the display shows an image but touch does not work, it is the **digitiser**. If the image is very dim but visible under bright light, it is the **backlight or inverter**, not the panel. If there is no image at all, it is the panel or the video connection. That three-way symptom split gets tested.

**SO-DIMM is not DIMM.** Laptop RAM is physically shorter. An option offering a desktop DIMM for a laptop upgrade is wrong on physical grounds.

**Mobile synchronisation methods** — to the cloud, to a desktop, or over a network — plus what data typically syncs (contacts, mail, calendar, photos, apps). Not difficult, but easy to skip entirely.

**Screen sharing, docking stations, and port replicators** appear, along with the peripherals you would actually plug into them.

## How to study it

If you have an old laptop, take it apart. Nothing in this domain survives contact with actually opening one — you will immediately understand why the RAM is a different shape, where the Wi-Fi card sits, and how the antenna cables route through the hinge.

If you do not, watch a teardown video for the same model twice. It is a distant second, but it is far better than reading.

Then learn the display symptom split (image but no touch / dim but visible / no image at all) and the antenna-in-the-lid fact. Those two account for a disproportionate share of the domain's questions.

At 15% this is a smaller domain, and it is genuinely one of the easier ones — but it is also the one candidates most often leave until the last week and then rush. Do not.
