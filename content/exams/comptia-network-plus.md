CompTIA Network+ validates vendor-neutral networking fundamentals: how networks are designed, addressed, secured, and troubleshot. N10-009 sits naturally between [A+](/exams/comptia-a-plus-core-1) and [Security+](/exams/comptia-security-plus) in the CompTIA path, and it's the certification that turns "I know computers" into "I understand how data actually moves".

## Who should take it

CompTIA recommends 9–12 months of networking experience plus A+ knowledge, but neither is enforced. Network+ suits helpdesk staff moving up, aspiring network and cloud engineers, and security-bound learners who want firmer footing before Security+. If you're committed to a Cisco environment specifically, compare it with the [CCNA](/exams/ccna-200-301), which is harder but deeper.

## How the exam is scored

Up to 90 questions in 90 minutes, scored 100–900 with a 720 pass mark. Alongside multiple choice you'll get performance-based questions — dragging network devices into a topology, matching ports to protocols, or working through a subnetting exercise.

## What to focus on

- **The OSI model as a working tool.** Not just the seven layer names, but which devices, protocols, and problems live at each layer — troubleshooting questions are frequently framed as "at which layer...?"
- **Subnetting, cold.** CIDR notation, calculating network/broadcast addresses and usable hosts, and VLSM. This is the exam's biggest skill gap; drill until a /26 or /28 takes you seconds, not minutes.
- **Ports and protocols.** The classic list — 22, 25, 53, 67/68, 80, 110, 123, 143, 161, 389, 443, 445, 3389 and friends — plus TCP vs UDP for each.
- **Routing and switching.** VLANs and trunking, STP's purpose, OSPF vs BGP at a concept level, and NAT/PAT.
- **Wireless.** Standards and frequencies, channel planning, and WPA2 vs WPA3.
- **Network operations and security.** Monitoring (SNMP, NetFlow), documentation, high-availability concepts, common attacks (VLAN hopping, rogue DHCP, on-path), and hardening basics.

## How to prepare

Plan five to eight weeks. Spend the first two on fundamentals and subnetting — everything else references them. Use a simulator like Packet Tracer to build small topologies: one VLAN-and-trunking lab teaches more than a chapter of reading. Then shift to daily mixed practice questions, always reading the explanation for wrong *and* right answers, because Network+ loves near-miss distractors (ARP vs RARP, STP vs RSTP, 802.11ac vs ax).

## Common pitfalls

Slow subnetting is the top exam-killer — it turns easy marks into time sinks. Second is fuzzy port knowledge; PBQs often demand exact matches. Third is skipping the "network operations" material (SLAs, documentation, change management) because it feels soft; it's weighted enough to swing a borderline result.

## After you pass

Network+ is valid for three years with continuing-education renewal. Most holders continue to [Security+](/exams/comptia-security-plus), which assumes and lightly re-tests this material, or go deeper into infrastructure with the [CCNA](/exams/ccna-200-301).
