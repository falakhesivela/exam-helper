The Cisco Certified Network Associate is the most respected associate-level networking certification, full stop. The 200-301 exam covers network fundamentals, IP connectivity and services, security fundamentals, and automation — with enough configuration-level depth that you cannot pass it from reading alone. That difficulty is exactly why it carries weight with employers.

## Who should take it

Cisco recommends a year or more of experience with Cisco solutions, but the CCNA has no prerequisites and is routinely earned by motivated beginners willing to put in lab hours. It suits network engineers (obviously), but also cloud and security engineers who want real networking depth — the subnetting, routing, and VLAN skills transfer everywhere. If you want a gentler, vendor-neutral start, look at [Network+](/exams/comptia-network-plus) first.

## How the exam is scored

Roughly 100 questions in 120 minutes. Cisco doesn't publish the passing score (it typically hovers in the 800s on a 300–1,000 scale) and you cannot go back to previous questions — once you submit an answer, it's final. That "no review" rule changes strategy: never leave a question unanswered, and don't burn ten minutes on one item hoping to return to it later.

## What to focus on

- **IP connectivity (the biggest domain).** Subnetting at speed, IPv6 addressing, static routing, OSPFv2 configuration and verification, and reading routing tables. Expect questions built on `show` command output.
- **Network access.** VLANs, trunking (802.1Q), inter-VLAN routing, EtherChannel, and spanning tree — including reading `show spanning-tree` output to identify root bridges and port roles.
- **IP services.** NAT configuration, NTP, DHCP, DNS, and quality-of-service concepts.
- **Security fundamentals.** Access control lists (the exam loves ACL logic), port security, DHCP snooping, dynamic ARP inspection, and WPA2/WPA3.
- **Automation and programmability.** REST APIs, JSON, controller-based networking, and Ansible/Terraform at a concept level — newer material many study plans undercook.

## Lab time is the exam

Get Packet Tracer (free) or CML and build constantly: configure OSPF between three routers, break it, fix it; set up VLANs and trunks; write ACLs and test them. CCNA questions frequently show partial configs or command output and ask what's wrong — pattern recognition you only develop with your hands on the CLI. A good rule: at least a third of your total study time should be in a lab.

## A 12-week framework

Weeks 1–3: fundamentals and subnetting until it's automatic. Weeks 4–6: switching (VLANs, STP) with daily labs. Weeks 7–9: routing (OSPF), IP services, and ACLs. Weeks 10–11: security, wireless, and automation. Week 12: full-length timed practice exams and weak-area repair. Working professionals should budget 8–10 hours a week.

## Common pitfalls

The no-going-back format punishes slow starters — warm up with a few practice questions the morning of the exam. Candidates also underestimate the automation domain, misread ACL wildcard masks (not the same as subnet masks!), and stall on questions requiring them to trace a packet's path through a topology. All three are fixable with targeted drills.
