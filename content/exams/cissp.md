The ISC2 CISSP is the gold-standard certification for security leadership. It spans eight domains — from risk management and asset security through cryptography, network security, identity, testing, operations, and software security — and consistently ranks among the highest-paying certifications in IT. It is not an entry-level exam, and it doesn't pretend to be.

## Who should take it

CISSP requires five years of cumulative, paid work experience across two or more of the eight domains (a four-year degree or approved certification like [Security+](/exams/comptia-security-plus) waives one year). You can pass the exam first and become an Associate of ISC2 while you accumulate the experience. The ideal candidate is a security engineer, consultant, or manager moving into architecture or leadership roles.

## How the exam works

The English-language exam is computer-adaptive: 100–150 questions in up to three hours, with the difficulty adjusting to your performance and no ability to revisit questions. Scoring is on a 1,000-point scale with 700 to pass — but because it's adaptive, the practical experience is that the exam keeps probing your weak domains until it's confident either way. The exam can end at 100 questions; that alone tells you nothing about whether you passed.

## Think like a manager, not an engineer

This is the single most important thing to understand about the CISSP. The exam rewards the *managerially correct* answer: human safety first, then risk assessment before action, then policy and process over technical quick fixes. When a question asks what you should do FIRST or what is MOST important, the engineer's instinct — patch it, block it, rebuild it — is often a distractor. The correct answer usually assesses, communicates, or follows the documented process. Practise catching yourself reaching for the technical fix.

## What to focus on

- **Domain 1 (Security and Risk Management) is the foundation.** Risk terminology and calculations, governance, legal and regulatory concepts, and business continuity. It's the heaviest-weighted domain and its mindset infects every other question.
- **Cryptography and PKI** at an architectural level — which mechanism provides confidentiality vs integrity vs non-repudiation, and how key management works.
- **Identity and access** — federation, SAML/OIDC, Kerberos, and access-control models (MAC, DAC, RBAC, ABAC) with their canonical use cases.
- **The software development security domain** is where technical candidates unexpectedly lose marks: SDLC models, change management, and software acquisition risk, all framed managerially.

## How to prepare

Most successful candidates study three to six months. Read broadly first — the CISSP punishes narrow expertise, and most people have two or three genuinely weak domains out of eight. Then drill practice questions in high volume, reviewing rationales even for correct answers, because learning to eliminate "true but not BEST" options is the core exam skill. In your final weeks, do full 125-question timed sessions to build the stamina the adaptive format demands.

## Common pitfalls

Answering as a technician (the classic failure mode), underestimating the legal/privacy material, and burning out mid-exam — three adaptive hours with no review is mentally taxing. Build stamina deliberately, and on exam day, trust your first managerial instinct.

## After you pass

Passing the exam isn't the end: you'll need your experience endorsed by an ISC2 member within nine months, and the certification requires 120 continuing-education credits over each three-year cycle. It's an investment — and it's the credential that most reliably opens doors to senior security roles.
